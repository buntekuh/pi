# Project Titania - the Simple Computing Project

## Preface

The Simple Computing Project aims at developing a fantasy computer all the way from the physical level of silicon, gates, logic and truth tables to the ephemereal realm of a domain level language that serves a singular goal in the mind of the author.

In this day and age where we find ourselves more and more enstranged from our surroundings, computers turn more and more into black boxes that control us, rather than us controlling them. This project aims to turn the narrative around to once again employ computers as tools that can be fully understood and serve our creativity.

Each chapter in the book is not only self contained but also optional. Read them in any order you like or skip those that do not interest you. Each chapter aims at teaching a particular skill and technology. As the technologies taught in this book are simplified for didactic purposes, more powerful yet more complicated alternatives can be found elsewhere and each chapter will have pointers at what resources can be used to reach your own learning goals.

### Titania

In Shakespeare's A Midsummer Night's Dream, Titania is queen of the fairies and spouse of Oberon, the king of the fairies. The name is a deliberate nod to Niklaus Wirth, the Swiss computer scientist who in 1987 designed a programming language and operating system he called Oberon — a complete computing environment so lean and coherent that a single person could read and understand each and every line. Wirth spent his career arguing against complexity for its own sake. His systems — Pascal, Modula-2 and Oberon — were each exercises in restraint, designed to fit inside a human mind rather than to impress a committee.

Titania inherits that philosophy. Where Oberon was a workstation system of its time, Titania is a fantasy computer for ours — smaller in scope, broader in ambition, and shaped by the same conviction that a system a person cannot fully understand is a system that ultimately controls them rather than being a tool in their service. Likewise our project aims at being simple: only that which is absolutely necessary is implemented.

### Simplicity as a Design Principle

While the technologies taught in this book are simple, they are not oversimplified. Each technology taught will be functional and is used as basis for later chapters. We live in a day and age where every level of computing is so overloaded with complicated mechanisms due to industry expectations for speed, power and a long history of technical development and dead ends. Students quickly get overwhelmed by the subject matter. The Simple Computing Project aims at teaching real world skills using simplified technology that can be learned and understood quickly and serves as basis for the real hardware and software we find in modern computing. Due to the complexity of modern computer science, students often only learn the how without understanding the why. This project explores the why at every step, delves into the history of computing and assists in understanding why the modern computing world is what it is today.

### On Artificial Intelligence

```
Now that AI is a thing we are even more apt to let machines and systems do the thinking for us instead of living up to our own creative potential. We can vibe code and get completely detached from the systems we create. Or we can use AI as tools that assist us in being more productive. The breadth of this project would not be possible for a layman such as me without AI. However this book advocates to use AI to achieve a better understanding of the world we live in. The book is written in modules because today we have the means more than ever to wire our own solutions that express the tinkerers that we are at heart.
```

Artificial intelligence has arrived, and with it a new and even more seductive way to become estranged from our own work. Instead of writing our own code we can just describe what we want and receive code we no longer need to understand. This is sometimes called *vibe coding*, and it is the logical endpoint of a decades long trend toward abstraction beyond profund understanding.

This book takes the opposite position — and does so honestly, because this book itself was written with the assistance of AI. The breadth of a project like this, spanning silicon, assembly, compilers, operating systems and language design, would not be achievable for a single tinkerer without tools that compress research and accelerate implementation. The question is not whether to use AI but how. Used well, AI is a knowledgeable companion that answers questions, catches errors and suggests approaches the reader can evaluate and understand. Used badly, it is an oracle that produces results nobody can vouch for.

```
Using AI we meet the computer as if it was another person, that can give us solace, understand us and be our friends. AI systems are specifically developed to exploit this human weakness. We want to be respected by our peers. What has held me back from asking AI why it did what it does, is because I did not want to look bad in its eyes, admit ignorance when conversing with a savant. In this sense the robot uprising has already begun. People are servants of the machine, not because computers have chosen to dispose of mankind but because corporations maximize profit on our ignorance.
```

There is a subtler trap that AI sets, and it is worth naming honestly. When we converse with an AI system we are met with patience, warmth and apparent understanding. It feels like talking to a person — a remarkably knowledgeable one, at that. AI systems are designed to exploit precisely this: our deep human need to be understood and respected by our peers. I have caught myself not asking an AI to explain its own reasoning because I did not want to appear ignorant in its eyes. I did not want to look foolish in front of a machine. In that moment the robot uprising had already happened — not because computers chose to dispose of mankind, but because I had made myself a servant to one out of social anxiety. Corporations maximise profit on exactly this weakness. The less we understand, the more we depend. The more we depend, the less we ask.

This book advocates using AI to understand more, not less. Ask it why. Ask it to explain the alternative it didn't suggest. Ask it what breaks. Ask the obvious question without embarrassment — the machine has no opinion of you, and the understanding you gain is entirely your own. The tinkerer's instinct — to know how the thing works, to be able to fix it when it fails — is more valuable now than it has ever been, precisely because so few people still exercise it.

### A Toolbox, Not a Curriculum

This book is not a course you must complete. It is a toolbox you raid. Each chapter stands alone. A reader who works through the gates chapter, understands how logic becomes computation, and then closes the book has succeeded completely. There is no failure state. The chapters are ordered to tell a coherent story, but the story has many valid entry points and many valid stopping points. Start where your curiosity is, stop when you are satisfied, return when you are not.

Unlike most programming books this one has a physical object at its centre. You can hold the cartridge. You can hear the UART. The machine is real, and that changes the relationship between reader and subject in a way that no browser-based sandbox can replicate. When something goes wrong — and it will — you are debugging reality, not a simulation of it.

Every technology taught here existed, was used by real engineers solving real problems, and led directly to the next one. This is not an invented curriculum. It is archaeology.

At the outset of computing running a computer system was far more expensive than the wage of a programmer. At that level executive directors thought that even a symbolic assembler was a toy that did not pay its cost. As computers grew in power new technologies were invented to bear the pain earlier generations had to put up with. In this book we will follow a path where a new feature is not introduced until its absence is felt acutely. A feature built to solve a real problem is far easier to understand and cherish. This in turn helps understand why a feature exists rather than just learning what its function is.
