Standard 5mm red LEDs, good choice. The specs tell you:

5mm — standard size
300mcd at 10mA — brightness at rated current (quite bright)

For your circuit, the key is the current limiting resistor. Red LEDs typically have a forward voltage of ~2.0V, and the Cmod A7 GPIO runs at 3.3V:


R = (3.3V - 2.0V) / I
You don't need the full 10mA — these will be very visible at 3-5mA. So:

Resistor	Current	Brightness
270Ω	~4.8mA	bright
330Ω	~3.9mA	good — recommended
470Ω	~2.8mA	slightly dimmer, still fine
330Ω is the classic choice and will look great. The FPGA GPIO pins are comfortable up to about 12mA, so you're well within limits.

Wiring per LED:


GPIO pin → 330Ω resistor → LED anode (+, longer leg) → LED cathode (-, shorter leg) → GND

which way does the large bit in the led go? toward ground or toward the resistor (phase)
The longer leg (anode, +) goes toward the resistor (the positive/power side), and the shorter leg (cathode, -) goes toward ground.


GPIO pin → resistor → [longer leg] LED [shorter leg] → GND
If you've already clipped the legs to the same length, most 5mm LEDs have a flat edge on the rim of the plastic body on the cathode (negative/ground) side.

### important

Important finding from the manual: The RGB LED is wired opposite to what you'd expect — its anodes connect to 3.3V and cathodes to the FPGA, so it lights up on logic LOW, not HIGH. Don't use it for the tutorial — it would need inverted logic. Stick with 5 breadboard LEDs.

For the 5 breadboard LEDs, use DIP pins 26–30 — they're right next to GND (pin 25) on the right side of the board, which makes wiring very tidy:

LEDS	DIP pin	Package pin
LEDS[0]	26	R3
LEDS[1]	27	T3
LEDS[2]	28	R2
LEDS[3]	29	T1
LEDS[4]	30	T2
GND	25	—
Wiring: DIP pin → resistor → LED (long leg) → LED (short leg) → GND rail → DIP pin 25.
